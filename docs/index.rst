Christoforos Kapsalis
=====================

**Technical writer for marketing-analytics and machine-learning products.**

I grew up in Agrinio, a small agricultural town in western Greece, where I
spent more hours on a mountain bike than anywhere else. Like most kids from the
countryside, the moment I turned eighteen I wanted out — to see somewhere
bigger. I worked for it, did well in the national entrance exams, and left for
Athens to study at the Athens University of Economics and Business. In a system
where attendance is optional, I did not miss a class.

That is where I first noticed something odd about myself. My interests pulled in
two directions I had been told did not belong together: I loved working with
data and finding the insight that explains a situation, and I loved sales,
marketing and communication. For a while I assumed I would have to choose one.

Instead I found the seam between them. My first real role was as "the data
person" in a marketing department, and I did well enough there that my employer
sponsored a master's in data science at the American College of Greece. It was
my first international environment, and it changed me — not only for the machine
learning and predictive analytics I came for, but for the people I met from all
over the world. All the way through it I kept working as a marketing analyst,
which is, more or less, what I have been ever since.

Being a marketing analyst meant living between two kinds of expertise. The data
half I had covered; the marketing half still had an academic gap I wanted to
close, because I wanted to be a whole professional rather than half of one. That
is what took me, on a Fulbright scholarship, to Syracuse University in upstate
New York for a master's in marketing.

And it was there, during the frozen Syracuse winter of 2026, with the rare gift
of free time to think, that the thing finally resolved. I realised that my love
for data and my love for marketing were never two interests balanced against
each other. They both sit on top of something older and simpler: a passion for
helping people do their best with the means they have — and, in my work, those
means are the software tools in front of them.

Once I saw it that way, I started looking for the work that lived at exactly
that point, and I kept arriving at the same title: technical writer. It fit
uncomfortably well. At my company documentation barely existed — our offering
was Power BI reports over a semantic model, and later a conversational layer on
top, and in that world documentation is usually an afterthought. But I could
watch marketers open our tools and get nothing out of them, not because the
tools were weak but because no one had helped them across. So I started writing
documentation on my own, part-time, from Syracuse. I got some things right and
plenty wrong, all on instinct — until I came across the `Diátaxis framework
<https://diataxis.fr>`_, created by Daniele Procida at Canonical, and my instinct
finally had a name and a shape.

Seven years of an unlikely academic path — economics, then data science, then
marketing — turn out to have been pointing at one thing all along. Economics
taught me to approach a problem whole, and in a structured way. Data science
taught me to read and test code, and to speak with the people who build it.
Marketing taught me to stand in someone else's shoes and start from what they
need. Technical writing is where all three finally do the same job at once:
helping people do their best with the tools they have.
